The true cost of manual order entry at a distributor (run your own numbers)

Every distributor knows order entry eats hours. Few have put their own numbers on it. Here is the math, a calculator to run it with your figures, and the two costs that never make it into the spreadsheet.

The labor math

The visible cost is simple multiplication: orders per day, times minutes per order, times what an ops hour actually costs you with taxes and benefits loaded in. Run your own numbers here. The defaults are just a starting point; drag them to match your desk.

Most distributors who run this land somewhere between "one full-time salary" and "a small department." And that's the smaller half of the cost.

The error cost

Re-keyed orders have typos. A transposed quantity, a wrong part number, a missed line item. Each one costs a return shipment, a credit memo, an annoyed customer, and an hour of someone untangling it. The industry doesn't agree on an exact error rate for manual entry, and we won't invent one, but you know your own returns-and-credits queue. Ask your team how many of last month's credit memos trace back to an entry mistake. That number, times what a correction cycle costs you, belongs on top of the labor math.

The cost that hurts most: the ceiling

Manual entry doesn't just cost money, it caps growth. Your desk can only key so many orders a day. Past that, queues build, ship dates slip, and the honest options are hire more people or turn work away. One industrial distributor we worked with was capped at roughly 100 orders a day for exactly this reason. After automating intake and the supplier legwork around it, the same team was processing 250 and more a day. The growth was already there in demand; the desk was the bottleneck.

What automating actually looks like

It does not mean replacing your ERP or your people. The pattern that works: orders arriving by email, PDF, or portal get parsed and validated automatically, clean ones flow straight into the ERP you already run, and your team reviews only the exceptions the system flags. People stop being typists and go back to being the judgment layer.

If your calculator number above made you wince, that's usually the sign it's worth a 30-minute look at how your orders come in today.
